China's International Relations

I study international relations, with a focus on Chinese foreign policy, international relations theory, and the international relations of East and Southeast Asia. My academic experience includes teaching at institutions across Australia, China, the United Kingdom, and the United States. My work explores the intersection of international relations, philosophy, and history, drawing on Chinese-Western comparative philosophy and East Asian history to illuminate contemporary Chinese foreign policy and international order. My long-term objective is to bridge Chinese and Western theories, aiming to deepen our understanding of international relations and explore avenues for a more stable global order.

Currently Associate Research Scholar and Lecturer at Yale University's MacMillan Center for International and Area Studies and Department of Political Science.

"A Neighbor of Neighbors: China's Policy toward Afghanistan, 1949-2024"

Comprehensive analysis of China's Afghanistan policy from the establishment of the PRC through the Taliban's return to power. Examines Chinese policy interests and goals and the dynamics of bilateral interactions and regional relations across seven decades.

Collaborative Project

"History, Lessons, Analogies: Learning from Wars and Pandemics"

Co-authored with Richard Ned Lebow. Examines how states and leaders learn (or fail to learn) from historical crises, wars, and pandemics. Asks why some lessons are learned and others not, why some become deeply embedded in a culture, what consequences this has beyond the domain in which they are thought to apply, and how lessons lose traction.

Ongoing Research

Toward Exemplary Competition: A New Framework for US-China Relations

Offers a new explanation of US-China rivalry by synthesizing the relational and exemplarist traditions in Chinese and Western thought. Emphasizes intellectual underpinning, psychological pathologies, and strategies of diplomacy and reassurance.
